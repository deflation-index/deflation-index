The Thesis

Technology creates exponential deflation through cost reductions in computing, communications, and energy. Over the past 34 years, these improvements should have made us 10-15x wealthier.

Yet purchasing power hasn't increased proportionally. The data shows a systematic gap: massive technological deflation (20-25% annually) is offset by monetary expansion (6-7% M2 growth), resulting in modest CPI inflation (2-3%).

This isn't accidental. Monetary policy is explicitly designed to prevent deflation—even when that deflation represents genuine technological abundance. The question: where does the value go?

The Timeline

Baseline year. Computing costs $10,000/GFLOPS. Long distance calls $0.25/min. Solar $20/watt. M2 money supply begins steady 6-7% annual growth.
2000
Internet mainstream. Computing deflation accelerates to 40-48% annually. Mobile phones proliferate. Communication costs falling rapidly. CPI remains 2-3%.
2008
Financial crisis triggers quantitative easing. Monetary base expands significantly. Technology continues exponential improvement trajectory. Divergence widens.
2015
Smartphones universal. Solar reaches grid parity in many markets. LEDs standard. Free communication via internet. Computing essentially costless at scale.
2020
COVID response: M2 expands 23.9% in one year. Computing power near-zero marginal cost. Energy costs plummeting. CPI surges 8%. Gap becomes visible.
2024
Solar now cheapest energy in human history (IEA). AI revolution. Battery costs down 98%. Computing productivity at historical highs. Purchasing power gap persists.

The Mechanism

Step 1: Technology improves exponentially. Costs collapse 20-25% annually across computing, communications, and energy.

Step 2: Monetary expansion (M2 growth of 6-7% annually) increases the money supply to maintain "price stability."

Step 3: New money enters the economy through financial system first (Cantillon Effect), flowing to assets and credit markets before reaching consumer goods.

Step 4: Result: CPI shows 2-3% inflation, appearing stable. However, this masks 20-25% technological deflation being offset by monetary expansion.

Step 5: Over 34 years: Technology delivered ~1,500% purchasing power gains. Actual purchasing power declined 45%. Net gap: ~2,000% or ~90% of potential abundance.
