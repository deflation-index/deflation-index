Methodology

How the Deflation Index is calculated: sector weights, data sources, formulas, and quality standards.

Overview

The Deflation Index tracks technological cost reductions across four fundamental sectors: computing, communications, energy, and transportation. Each sector is measured using cost-per-performance metrics that capture the true force of technological progress.

The Master DI is a weighted geometric mean of these four sector indices, showing how technological deflation compounds over time.

Master DI = (Computing^0.25 × Communications^0.20 × Energy^0.25 × Transportation^0.15 × Future^0.15)

Why geometric mean? Technological improvements multiply (compound), they don't add. A geometric mean properly captures this multiplicative relationship.

Calculation Method

Step 1: Measure Cost-Per-Performance

For each component within each sector, we track the cost per unit of performance over time:

  • Computing: Cost per billion floating-point operations per second ($/GFLOPS)
  • Communications: Cost per gigabyte of data transmission ($/GB)
  • Energy: Levelized cost of electricity from solar ($/kWh)
  • Transportation: Battery pack cost per kilowatt-hour ($/kWh)

Step 2: Calculate Component Indices

Each component is indexed to 100 in the base year (1990 for most, 2010 for energy/transport). As costs fall, the index falls proportionally.

Component Index = (Cost in Current Year / Cost in Base Year) × 100

Step 3: Weight Components Within Sectors

Multiple components within each sector are combined using geometric means and their respective weights. For example, Computing = (GFLOPS^0.60 × Storage^0.30 × RAM^0.10).

Step 4: Combine Sectors Into Master DI

The four sector indices are combined using the Master DI formula shown above, producing a single comprehensive measure of technological deflation.

Step 5: Calculate Annual Rates

Year-over-year percentage changes show the annual deflation rate. The average annual rate (1990-2024) is -5.2%.

Conservative Assumptions

Where uncertainty exists, we choose the more conservative estimate. This means the true technological deflation is likely higher than we measure. The index understates the gap, not overstates it.

Understanding The Gaps

We measure two related gaps that reveal how technological gains flow through the economy:

The Deflation Gap (239pp cumulative, 7.8pp annual)

What it measures: Deflation that should have reached consumers but didn't.

Deflation Gap = |Tech Deflation| + CPI Inflation
= 84pp + 155pp = 239pp

Technology delivered 84% deflation. Consumer prices rose 155% instead. The 239 percentage point gap represents "missing deflation."

The Abundance Gap (545pp cumulative)

What it measures: Total economic force captured rather than passed to consumers.

Abundance Gap = |Tech Deflation| + M2 Expansion - CPI Inflation
= 84pp + 615pp - 155pp = 545pp

This is the comprehensive measure: technology pushed down (84pp), money printing pushed up (615pp), consumer prices rose 155pp. The 545pp gap represents wealth that flowed elsewhere—to corporate profits, asset prices, and complexity costs.

Both gaps are valid and tell complementary stories. The Deflation Gap focuses on consumer experience. The Abundance Gap includes the full monetary context.
