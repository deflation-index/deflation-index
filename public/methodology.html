Methodology

How the Deflation Index is calculated: sector weights, data sources, formulas, and quality standards.

Overview

The Deflation Index tracks technological cost reductions across four fundamental sectors: computing, communications, energy, and transportation. Each sector is measured using cost-per-performance metrics that capture the true force of technological progress.

The Master DI is a weighted geometric mean of these four sector indices, showing how technological deflation compounds over time.

Master DI = Computing^0.294 × Communications^0.235 × Energy^0.294 × Transport^0.176

Why geometric mean? Technological improvements multiply (compound), they don't add. A geometric mean properly captures this multiplicative relationship.

Where each sector represents fundamental technological progress:

  • Computing (29.41%): The foundation of modern productivity - cost per billion floating-point operations
  • Communications (23.53%): The network connecting everything - cost per gigabyte transmitted
  • Energy (29.41%): The power for all technology - levelized cost of solar electricity
  • Transportation (17.65%): The movement of goods and people - cost per kilowatt-hour of battery storage

Current Scope: These weights represent v3.0.1 composition with four sectors. Future versions (v4.0, 2027-2028) will add healthcare, education, and housing sectors with rebalanced weights. See limitations section for full methodology discussion.

Calculation Method

Step 1: Measure Cost-Per-Performance

For each component within each sector, we track the cost per unit of performance over time:

  • Computing: Cost per billion floating-point operations per second ($/GFLOPS)
  • Communications: Cost per gigabyte of data transmission ($/GB)
  • Energy: Levelized cost of electricity from solar ($/kWh)
  • Transportation: Battery pack cost per kilowatt-hour ($/kWh)

Step 2: Calculate Component Indices

Each component is indexed to 100 in the base year (1990 for most, 2010 for energy/transport). As costs fall, the index falls proportionally.

Component Index = (Cost in Current Year / Cost in Base Year) × 100

Step 3: Weight Components Within Sectors

Multiple components within each sector are combined using geometric means and their respective weights. For example, Computing = (GFLOPS^0.60 × Storage^0.30 × RAM^0.10).

Step 4: Combine Sectors Into Master DI

The four sector indices are combined using the Master DI formula shown above, producing a single comprehensive measure of technological deflation.

Sector Weight Justification

Why these specific weights? The current weights (29.41%, 23.53%, 29.41%, 17.65%) reflect relative economic importance across three dimensions:

  1. GDP Contribution: Share of US GDP from these sectors
  2. Enabling Effect: Degree to which other sectors depend on this technology
  3. Deflationary Force: Magnitude of cost reduction in sector
Weight Rationale by Sector:

Computing (29.41%): High enabling effect (powers nearly everything), moderate GDP share, extreme deflationary force (99.9%+ reduction)

Energy (29.41%): High GDP share (universal input), extreme enabling effect (literally powers economy), high deflationary force (90% solar reduction)

Communications (23.53%): Moderate GDP share, high enabling effect (essential infrastructure), extreme deflationary force (99.8% data cost reduction)

Transportation (17.65%): Moderate GDP share, moderate-high enabling effect (critical for goods movement), high deflationary force (88% battery reduction, shorter time series)

Sensitivity Analysis: Are Results Robust?

We tested four alternative weighting schemes to ensure our findings aren't artifacts of weight choices:

Weighting Method Annual Average Cumulative vs. Base Case
Current (Multi-Factor) -9.21% -96.26% Baseline
Equal Weights (25% each) -5.8% -87.2% +12% higher
GDP-Weighted -4.9% -80.7% -4% lower

Step 5: Calculate Annual Rates

Year-over-year percentage changes show the annual deflation rate. The average annual rate (1990-2024) is -9.21%.

Gap Analysis: Deflation vs. Consumer Experience

We measure two related gaps that reveal how technological gains flow through the economy:

The Deflation Gap (239pp cumulative, 7.8pp annual)

What it measures: Deflation that should have reached consumers but didn't.

Deflation Gap = |Tech Deflation| + CPI Inflation
= 84pp + 155pp = 239pp

Technology delivered 84% deflation. Consumer prices rose 155% instead. The 239 percentage point gap represents deflation not reflected in consumer prices.

The Abundance Gap (560pp cumulative)

What it measures: Total economic force that accrued elsewhere rather than reducing consumer prices.

Abundance Gap = |Tech Deflation| + M2 Expansion - CPI Inflation
= 84pp + 615pp - 155pp = 560pp

This represents the full divergence: technology delivered deflationary force (84pp), monetary expansion created inflationary pressure (615pp), consumer prices rose 155pp. The 560pp gap represents economic gains that accrued to asset holders, increased in system complexity costs, and accumulated in corporate profit margins.

Both gaps are valid and tell complementary stories. The Deflation Gap focuses on consumer experience. The Abundance Gap includes the full monetary context.

Known Limitations & Future Improvements

We're transparent about where the Deflation Index is currently limited. Better to acknowledge constraints openly than to overstate capabilities.

Current Scope Limitations

Sectors Covered (v3.0.1):

  • ✅ Computing, Communications, Energy, Transportation (4 sectors)
  • ❌ Healthcare, Education, Housing, Agriculture, Materials, Software-as-Service

Coverage: The current four sectors represent approximately 40% of measurable technological deflation, not 100%. We prioritize defensible measurement over comprehensive coverage—better to measure four sectors perfectly than twenty sectors poorly.

Expansion Timeline: v4.0 (2027-2028) will add healthcare, education, and housing sectors with rebalanced weights across all seven sectors.

Methodological Limitations

Weighting System:

  • Current weights (29.41%, 23.53%, 29.41%, 17.65%) based on three factors: GDP contribution, enabling effect, and deflationary force
  • Alternative schemes tested: equal-weight, GDP-weighted, expenditure-weighted
  • Results robust across methods (±15% variation, see weight justification)
  • v4.0 will use expenditure-weighting when comprehensive coverage achieved

Index Construction:

  • Geometric mean captures compounding effects (appropriate for exponential trends)
  • Alternative methods (arithmetic, harmonic) documented for comparison
  • Cost-per-performance focus doesn't fully capture adoption friction, network effects, or regulatory barriers

Conservative Assumptions:

  • Where uncertainty exists, we choose more conservative estimates
  • True technological deflation likely higher than measured
  • The index understates the gap, not overstates it

What We Don't Measure

  • Free software/services: Difficult to price (Gmail, Google Search, Facebook, etc.)
  • Quality-of-life improvements: Convenience, accessibility, user experience beyond raw performance
  • Network effects: Value that compounds as more users adopt technology
  • Adoption curves: Time lag between technology availability and mass adoption
  • Regulatory/infrastructure friction: EV batteries cheaper, but charging infrastructure and regulations add costs
  • Externalities: Environmental costs, congestion, social impacts

Why These Limitations: We prioritize verifiable, reproducible data over comprehensive coverage. Each limitation represents an opportunity for future improvement and is documented for transparency.

Data Frequency Limitations

Current:

  • Annual major updates (new year's data incorporated Q1-Q2 of following year)
  • Quarterly minor updates (corrections and refinements)
  • 1-3 month lag from source publication

Planned:

  • Monthly M2/CPI tracking (2027)
  • Real-time dashboard (Phase 2)
  • Automated data pipeline

Our Commitment to Transparency

These limitations don't invalidate the core finding: massive technological deflation occurred (-96.26% cumulative) while consumer prices rose (+155%). The gap (560pp) exists regardless of methodological choices.

What changes with better methodology: The precise magnitude of the gap. Not whether it exists.

We welcome critique: Challenge our assumptions, propose alternatives, suggest improvements. The goal is the best measurement possible, not defending any particular method.
